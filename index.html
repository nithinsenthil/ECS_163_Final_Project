Environmental Inequality in California

Where do pollution and health burdens overlap across California counties?

Environmental inequality happens when pollution and environmental burdens are not distributed evenly across communities. California is often discussed through its major cities, coastlines, agriculture, and natural landscapes, but environmental burden does not affect every county in the state the same way. Some counties face higher exposure to pollution-related factors, may make communities more vulnerable to long-term environmental stress.

Monterey County is located along the pacific coastline and is well known for its marine life, scenery, and agriculture. In places like Monterey County, environmental concerns may not always be obvious at a first glance. Despite being known for its beauty, pesticide exposure remains an important environmental concern throughout it’s communities. This example helps introduce a larger question: how do pollution, health, and socioeconomic burdens overlap across California counties?

This visualization uses CalEnviroScreen 4.0¹, a 2021 environmental justice dataset from California’s Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment². This dataset brings together information on pollution exposure, environmental conditions, health indicators, demographics, and socioeconomic factors across California communities. Rather than looking at one issue alone, this walkthrough explores how different burdens can appear together throughout our state. Pollution burden³, lead exposure, asthma, and geography each tell part of the story. When viewed together, they help show how environmental inequality can take shape through overlapping environmental and health conditions.

Which counties carry the highest pollution burden?

Pollution burden scores are not evenly distributed across California. Some counties across California carry noticeably higher average pollution burden³ scores, while others fall lower on the scale. These differences in counties pollution burden scores reflect how exposure to pollution-related factors, including factors such as air pollution, pesticide exposure, traffic pollution, water quality, and other environmental conditions vary across the state. In the bar chart below, each county is represented by an individual bar. The height shows the county’s average pollution burden³ score. Counties with higher bars experience higher average pollution burden, while counties with lower bars experience lower average pollution burden. This gives a broad sense of where pollution burden is more concentrated before moving into the social conditions that may overlap with the environmental challenges they face.

The bar chart shows us where the pollution burden is highest, but pollution alone does not tell the full story. To better understand environmental inequality, we also need to examine whether pollution burden overlaps with health concerns.

Do pollution and health burdens appear together?

Environmental inequality also has a health component to it. Pollution burden is only one part of the story. Environmental exposure can overlap with health concerns, especially in communities where people may already face higher risks from pollution-related conditions. Lead exposure and asthma are two important health-related indicators because they show how environmental conditions may connect to physical health burdens. The scatterplot below compares lead exposure with asthma rates across California counties. Each point represents one county. Counties positioned farther to the right have higher lead exposure, while counties positioned higher on the graph have higher asthma rates. When lead exposure and asthma rates appear high in the same county, it suggests that environmental and health burdens may be overlapping. This does not prove that lead exposure directly causes asthma, but it helps reveal where multiple concerns may be concentrated at the same time.

The scatterplot helps us compare counties numerically, but it does not show exactly where these counties are located. To add a geographic perspective, our map allows users to explore how pollution and health burdens are distributed across the state.

Where are these burdens located?

County-level patterns become clearer when they are placed back onto the map of California. Pollution, health, and socioeconomic indicators may appear more concentrated in certain regions, or more unevenly across the state. The choropleth map below allows different county-level indicators geographically. Darker colors represent higher values, while lighter colors represent lower values. By switching between variables, the map shows how pollution, health, and socioeconomic burdens appear across different parts of California. Hovering over a county reveals more detailed values for that location, making it possible to compare one county’s conditions with the broader statewide pattern.

Environmental inequality appears through overlapping burdens. Together, these views show that environmental inequality cannot be understood through the lens of one variable alone. Pollution burden shows where environmental stress is higher, the scatterplot shows how health-related indicators such as lead exposure and asthma may overlap, and the map shows how these patterns appear geographically across California. These patterns should be interpreted as overlap and correlation, not direct causation. The goal is to make the data easier to explore and to better understand where environmental and health burdens may be concentrated across California counties.

¹ CalEnviroScreen 4.0: A California environmental justice dataset that combines pollution, health, demographic, and socioeconomic indicators to identify communities experiencing higher cumulative burdens. ² Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment: The California agency that created and maintains CalEnviroScreen, and focuses on protecting and enhancing the health of California's environment and citizens through evaluations that help support regulations. ³ Pollution burden: A measure of environmental stress from pollution sources and polluted conditions, such as air pollution, pesticides, traffic pollution, groundwater threats, and other environmental indicators.
