Environmental Inequality in California

Where do pollution and health burdens overlap across California counties?

Pollution does not affect every California county equally. Across California, some counties experience higher environmental burdens, including air pollution, pesticide exposure, and lead exposure. These burdens can overlap with health and socioeconomic challenges, making some communities more vulnerable than others. Pollution burden is not always visible. For example, Monterey county is known for its beautiful natural scenery, but its agricultural landscape makes pesticide exposure an important concern for its residents.

Environmental Inequality refers to communities of lower-income pollution and environmental health risks at disproportionate rates compared to others. In California specifically, we see that there are counties that face higher pollution rates while also facing socioeconomic challenges such as poverty, unemployment, and low-education levels. These burdens that communities across the state face can make them more vulnerable to negative health outcomes. Our project builds on the work conducted by the OEHHA and their CalEnviroScreen by creating a visualization dashboard that allows our audience to explore how pollution, health, and poverty burdens overlap from county-to-county across the state.

Which counties carry the highest pollution burden?

This chart ranks the counties by average pollution burden. Counties with longer bars have higher average pollution burden scores. These counties can be used as a starting point for exploring where environmental inequality may be more concentrated.

The bar chart shows us where the pollution burden is highest, but pollution alone does not tell the full story. To better understand environmental inequality, we also need to examine whether pollution burden overlaps with health concerns.

Do pollution and health burdens appear together?

Each point on the scatterplot represents a California county. This chart compares pollution burden with a health indicator, such as asthma, to see whether higher pollution burden overlaps with higher health burden. If counties with higher pollution burden also tend to appear higher on the health burden axis, this may suggest that environmental and health burdens overlap in some areas.

The scatterplot helps us compare counties numerically, but it does not show exactly where these counties are located. To add a geographic perspective, our map allows users to explore how pollution and health burdens are distributed across the state.

Where are these burdens located?

This map displays how environmental and health burdens vary across California counties. Darker colors represent higher burden, while lighter colors represent lower burden. Users can hover over each county to see more details, including pollution burden, health burden indicators, and socioeconomic measures.

Together, the bar chart, scatterplot, and map show that environmental inequality is not only about pollution levels. It is also about how pollution, health, and socioeconomic burdens overlap across places. Our overall goal is not to claim direct causation, but to identify patterns, compare counties, and better understand where environmental and health burdens may be concentrated in California.
